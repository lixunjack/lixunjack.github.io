Listening to Rocks Breathe: A View of Nonlinear Elasticity in Granular Solids

When we’re first taught elasticity, we usually meet Hooke’s law: double the stress, double the strain. Springs behave nicely, waves pass through without changing shape, and everything stays linear and predictable.

Real rocks don’t play by those rules. Sandstones, chalk, concrete, and crushed fault rocks are made of tiny grains and cracks (Figure 1). At their contacts, grains are constantly opening, closing, and slipping past each other as the rock is shaken or stressed. At small vibrations they act almost linearly, but once we shake them a bit harder – at levels similar to strong seismic waves – they start to show a very different, “nonlinear” personality.

Over the last few decades, lab experiments have shown that granular rocks behave in surprisingly complicated ways once we drive them hard enough with seismic‑like vibrations. Their resonant frequencies shift as the drive increases, their stiffness can drop almost instantly when we change the load, and then recover only very slowly. Their response also “remembers” what happened before: it depends on the history of the deformation, not just what we are doing to the sample right now.

One of the simplest ways to see this is to put a rock sample in a resonance experiment, turn up the shaking, and watch how its favourite vibration frequency moves. In a linear world, the resonance peak would simply get taller as we shake harder, but its frequency would stay put. In the lab, we see something else entirely (Figure 2).

To dig deeper into this behaviour, laboratory researchers have combined two operations:

  • Drive the rock in resonance to create controlled, relatively large strains, and
  • Probe its stiffness with small, high‑frequency waves while it is being shaken.

This is what Dynamic Acousto‑Elastic Testing (DAET) and Nonlinear Resonant Ultrasound Spectroscopy (NRUS) do:

  • NRUS: track the resonance peak as drive increases.
  • DAET: monitor high‑frequency wave speed during low‑frequency pumping and recovery.
A low‑frequency source makes the sample ring in one of its resonant modes, while a high‑frequency ultrasonic probe continuously measures how the wave speed – and therefore the stiffness – changes as the rock is stretched and squeezed. The result is a kind of “stress–strain loop” for the wave speed, revealing conditioning, hysteresis, and other fast nonlinear effects (Figure 3).

Another striking feature is that rocks can take a very long time to recover after being strongly shaken. If we “condition” a sample by driving it hard for a while, its seismic velocity drops. When we stop shaking, the velocity does not bounce back right away. Instead, it creeps back logarithmically with time – fast at first, then slower and slower, over minutes to hours (Figure 4).

This is not just a lab curiosity. At the scale of real faults and basins, the shallow crust shows very similar behaviour after strong earthquakes. Immediately after a big event, seismic velocities in the upper crust drop measurably; in the weeks to years that follow, they gradually climb back toward their pre‑event values. Noise‑based monitoring of the Parkfield segment of the San Andreas Fault is a good example (Figure 5).

1) Why Rocks Don’t Obey Hooke’s Law

Hooke’s law says that stress is proportional to strain as long as deformations remain “small”. That works well for many engineering materials, and it underpins most of exploration and global seismology: we model seismic waves as small perturbations travelling through a linear elastic Earth.

Granular solids – think sandstones, concrete, fault gouge, porous chalk – are different. They are made of stiff mineral grains glued together by weaker material, riddled with microcracks, grain contacts, and bonds. These internal defects are small (from nanometres up to microns) but numerous, and they dominate the material’s compliance and attenuation.

Experiments have shown that:

  • Nonlinearity kicks in at extremely small strains, sometimes as low as $ 10^{-7} $–$ 10^{-6} $, far below the threshold where you would expect classical lattice anharmonicity to matter.
  • The response is strongly asymmetric: compression and extension do not behave the same. Compression can close cracks and increase stiffness; tension can open contacts and cause dramatic softening.
  • The stress–strain relation shows hysteresis and memory: the path taken (loading vs unloading) matters, and the material “remembers” past excursions.

2) A Mathematical Playground: The Duffing Oscillator

Before tackling real rocks, a simpler nonlinear system – the Duffing oscillator – shows how asymmetry and cliffs can arise from strong softening alone:

$ \ddot{x} + \phi \dot{x} + k x + \delta x^3 = A_r \sin(\omega t). $

As $ | \delta | $ or $ A_r $ grows, the resonance peak shifts, tilts, and ultimately folds over to create a vertical “cliff” where tiny frequency changes trigger large amplitude jumps (a bifurcation).

3) A Thermodynamic Picture: Fractures as Bistable Elements

We follow the Physical Review B model (Li, Sens‑Schönfelder & Snieder, 2018): each microcontact/fracture is bistable (open or closed) with an energy barrier $ E_b $ between states. Thermal activation drives switching with Arrhenius rates:

$$ \tau_o = A \, e^{(E_b - E_o)/k_B T}, \quad \tau_c = A \, e^{(E_b - E_c)/k_B T}. $$

For contacts with the same barrier energy, the fraction of open fractures $ n_o $ evolves as:

$$ \frac{d n_o}{dt} = - \frac{n_o}{\tau_o} + \frac{1 - n_o}{\tau_c}, \quad n_{\mathrm{eq}} = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\big[(E_o - E_c)/k_B T\big]}. $$

The state energies depend smoothly on dynamic strain $ \\varepsilon $ via sigmoids (extension favors opening; compression favors closing):

$$ E_o(\varepsilon) = B_0 - \frac{A_1}{1 + \exp[-(\varepsilon - \mu)/\sigma]}, \quad E_c(\varepsilon) = A_0 + \frac{A_1}{1 + \exp[-(\varepsilon - \mu)/\sigma]}. $$

We assume a uniform spectrum of barrier energies $ E_b \\in [E_{b,\\min}, E_{b,\\max}] $ to represent many time scales; the sample‑averaged open fraction is $ N_o(t) $ (average of $ n_o $ over $ E_b $). Stiffness then follows a linearized damage law:

$$ Y(t) = Y_0 - C_0 \big( N_o(t) - N_{\mathrm{ori}} \big). $$

This closes a feedback loop: strain → energies → switching → $ N_o(t) $ → $ Y(t) $ → strain.

4) Simulating Real Resonance Experiments

The PRB study reproduces the laboratory protocol step‑by‑step. A driven linear oscillator is coupled to the evolving modulus:

$$ \\ddot{R}_A + 2 \\gamma \\dot{R}_A + \\Omega^2(t) R_A = A_r e^{-i \\omega t}, \\quad \\Omega(t) = \\frac{\\pi}{L_0} \\sqrt{\\frac{Y(t)}{\\rho}}. $$

The measurable response is the acceleration amplitude $ \\omega^2 R_A L_0 $, after holding each frequency for a finite sweep interval $ \\Delta t $ to let $ N_o(t) $ and $ Y(t) $ evolve. The frequency is then stepped by $ \\Delta f $ up or down; the final state at one step is the initial state for the next.

A key timescale is the maximum relaxation time $ \\tau_{\\max} $ (set by the slowest contacts):

$$ \\tau_{\\max}^{-1} = \\left( \\tau_o^{-1} + \\tau_c^{-1} \\right)\\big|_{E_b = E_{b,\\max}}. $$

Sweep‑rate regimes: “fast” means $ \\Delta t \\ll \\tau_{\\max} $ (strong memory); “slow” means $ \\Delta t \\gg \\tau_{\\max} $ (near‑equilibrium at each step).

5) What the Model Reproduces: Slow Dynamics, Asymmetry, Hysteresis, and Cliffs

5.1 Slow Dynamics: Log‑Time Recovery

After strong conditioning, the resonant frequency recovers approximately linearly with $ \\log t $ over decades in time, matching classic NRUS/DAET observations. This emerges from superposing many Arrhenius processes across $ E_b $; a single‑barrier case recovers roughly exponentially (no log‑time).

5.2 Asymmetric Resonance Curves

Increasing drive opens more contacts near resonance, softens the sample, and shifts $ \\Omega $ downward while you are measuring it. This feedback steepens the left flank and flattens the right flank of the resonance peak—an asymmetry reproduced even without multi‑scale relaxation.

5.3 Up–Down Differences and Sweep‑Rate Dependence

With weak to moderate nonlinearity, the up–down difference is a memory effect from slow dynamics: it vanishes when $ \\Delta t \\gtrsim \\tau_{\\max} $ (slow sweeps).

5.4 Cliffs and Bifurcations in Strongly Nonlinear Regimes

Increase the damage sensitivity $ C_0 $ or the drive and the resonance curve folds, creating a vertical “cliff”. In this regime, two stable equilibrium states can coexist at the same frequency; the system follows one branch until it reaches a turning point and then jumps—hence the persistent up–down difference right at the cliff, even for slow sweeps.

5.5 Hysteresis and Oscillating Modulus

Under cyclic loading, rate‑dependent switching produces hysteretic modulus–strain loops (“butterflies”), consistent with DAET.

6) What This Tells Us About Rocks, Faults, and Reservoirs

Key takeaways:

  • Mesoscopic contacts and fractures dominate nonlinearity at microstrain levels.
  • Log‑time recovery reflects a broad spectrum of activation energies and time scales.
  • Cliffs are the observable signature of a bifurcation in the coupled contact–oscillator system.
  • Field‑scale transients (post‑seismic or injection‑induced) are consistent with the same physics.

7) Where Next? Extending the Model and Taking It to the Field

  • Include fluids and pressure (capillarity, effective stress), and temperature.
  • Couple to rate‑and‑state friction to bridge lab resonance and fault‑zone transients.
  • Embed in monitoring (e.g., crosswell or DAS time‑lapse) to invert for evolving $ N_o(t) $ or $ Y(t) $.
  • Targeted tests: at a fixed frequency within the bifurcation band, prepare different initial damage states to show two distinct stable equilibria.
