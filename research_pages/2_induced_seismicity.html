Induced Seismicity at the UK “Hot Dry Rock” Test Site: Diffusivity, Anisotropy, and Aseismic Slip

How much of a geothermal reservoir’s deformation is felt as earthquakes—and how much slips by silently? We revisited a classic UK “hot dry rock” experiment at Rosemanowes (Cornwall) and analysed 5,184 induced microearthquakes recorded during the main stimulation phase. Using simple but powerful metrics—like the seismic strain partition factor and the seismogenic index—we found that only a tiny fraction of injected strain was released seismically. Meanwhile, the growing cloud of event locations mapped out an anisotropic hydraulic diffusivity tensor with exceptionally strong vertical permeability. Putting the pieces together suggests a coherent mechanism: shear slip on optimally oriented pre-existing joints, coupled with widespread aseismic opening and creep that channel fluid vertically.

Paper: Li, X., Main, I., & Jupe, A. (2018). Induced seismicity at the UK ‘hot dry rock’ test site for geothermal energy production. Geophysical Journal International, 214, 331–344. https://doi.org/10.1093/gji/ggy135

1) Site and Geological Background

The Rosemanowes Quarry lies on the Carnmenellis granite in southwest UK, part of the Cornubian batholith. The HDR concept targeted hot, low-permeability crystalline rock at ~2 km depth. Stimulation used elevated-pressure injection to increase connectivity and flow. Downhole accelerometers and calibration surveys delivered a high-quality microseismic catalogue in the 1980s—later reprocessed to modern standards.

2) Injection, Production, and A Decelerating Seismic Response

Fluid injection (VI) and production (VP) define the net volume stored, VN = VI − VP. We compared the rate of seismic moment release to the rate of net injection in 30 h windows to estimate an apparent shear modulus μap and a strain partition factor η = μap/μ. Two key results emerged:

  • η ≈ 10−4 (≈0.01%) → deformation was overwhelmingly aseismic.
  • A step-like drop in μap with ongoing injection (near net volume ≈ 70,600 m³) indicating a deceleration of seismic response as storage increased.

3) Statistical Properties: Gutenberg–Richter and Seismogenic Index

The frequency–magnitude distribution follows a Gutenberg–Richter law with a high b-value: b ≈ 2.28 ± 0.21 (Mc ≈ −1.0), suggesting low effective stress concentration. Using Shapiro’s framework, we estimated a seismogenic index Σ ≈ −4.2 (for Mc ≈ −0.8), on the low side for geothermal projects and consistent with the very small seismic strain fraction.

4) Flow Models: From Isotropic Diffusion to Anisotropic Channeling

If pore-pressure relaxes in a uniform isotropic medium, the triggering front radius scales as r ∝ t1/2. Instead, the mean event distance here scales as r ∝ t0.382±0.017—a hallmark of non-Fickian diffusion. Fitting an ellipsoidal triggering front yields principal apparent diffusivities:

  • D11 ≈ 0.01 m²/s (minor axis, ~NE)
  • D22 ≈ 0.03 m²/s (intermediate axis, ~NW)
  • D33 ≈ 1.6 m²/s (major axis, vertical)

The vertical component dwarfs the horizontal—implying a strongly permeable vertical channel formed or activated during stimulation.

5) Fracture Networks, Stress, and Focal Mechanisms

Downhole imaging shows two subvertical joint sets: a NW–SE set (Set 1) and an orthogonal NE–SW set (Set 2). Present-day maximum horizontal stress (σH) aligns ~NW–SE—close to the major horizontal axis of the seismic cloud. Composite focal mechanisms are dominantly double-couple strike-slip (some normal-faulting), consistent with shear reactivation.

6) A Consistent Triggering Mechanism

The observations are jointly explained by:

  • Shear slip on optimally oriented pre-existing subvertical joints (Set 1, ~NW–SE) → recorded microseismicity.
  • Aseismic opening and creep that dominate the strain budget (η ≈ 10−4), reducing μap with ongoing injection.
  • Activated tensile conduits (e.g., Set 2 openings) that boost vertical permeability, producing strong anisotropic diffusivity.

7) Implications for Geothermal Operations and Risk

  • Most deformation can be silent. Low η and Σ imply that large volumetric strain accrues with little seismic release.
  • Expect anisotropic flow. Strong vertical permeability suggests fracture networks can funnel fluid upward/downward efficiently.
  • Seismic response can decelerate. The step-like drop in μap indicates evolving storage and connectivity during operations.
  • Monitoring priorities. Joint-set orientation, in-situ stress, and vertical connectivity become key for forecasting and mitigation.
